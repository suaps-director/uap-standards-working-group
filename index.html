UAP Standards of Evidence Working Group

Establishing a unified scientific framework for the study of Unidentified Anomalous Phenomena.

Working Group Organization

To ensure a rigorous, transparent, and multi-disciplinary approach, the UAP Standards of Evidence Working Group will be structured to foster broad academic collaboration. Drawing inspiration from the successful framework of NASA's astrobiology workshops, our proposed leadership model is designed for comprehensive oversight and focused expertise.

Proposed Leadership Structure

  • Steering Committee: A diverse body of senior academics and researchers from relevant fields (e.g., physics, engineering, sociology, philosophy of science) will provide high-level strategic guidance and ensure the project's scientific integrity.
  • Project Coordinator: A central administrative lead responsible for managing day-to-day operations, facilitating communication between committees, and guiding the project toward its objectives.
  • Sub-Committee Leads: Each of the specialized sub-committees will be chaired by a recognized expert in that domain, responsible for directing their group's research and contributing to the final white paper.
  • External Advisory Board: An independent group of experts will be invited to review the working group's findings and provide critical feedback, ensuring the final standards meet the highest levels of academic scrutiny.

The Need for a Standardized Approach

In the nascent field of scientific research into "unidentified anomalous-aerospace phenomena," there currently exists no uniform, widely accepted framework for conducting research on a non-forensic basis. While past case reports are an important resource, they are preliminary to the work of measuring the physical characteristics of UAP with modern observational science.

It is only through a body of instrumented observations from well-calibrated assets that we can transition from a dependency on eyewitness testimony to a state of epistemological independence, where data can stand on its own. This marks a clear division between retrospective investigation and active, observational field science.

This lack of clear standards was a challenge previously faced by the astrobiology community. Their solution was to form a dedicated working group that produced the Community Workshop Report from the Biosignature Standards of Evidence Workshop, a document that received independent review from the National Academies. Inspired by this precedent, our project aims to fill the same critical gap for UAP studies.

Proposed Sub-Committees

To address the multifaceted nature of UAP studies, the working group will form several focused sub-committees:

Physical Sciences

Focus on observational methods, instrumentation, and standards for physical data collection.

Materials Analysis

Establish standards of evidence for analyzing potential materials of non-terrestrial origin.

Biological & Life Sciences

Investigate and standardize the study of reported biological or physiological effects on witnesses.

Social & Human Sciences

Analyze the cultural, political, and sociological dimensions of the UAP/human interface.

Witness Testimony & Epistemology

Examine the evidentiary import and philosophical status of first-person ("subjective") human experience.

Conceptual Foundations & The "Anomaly" Question

A core task is to define what constitutes an "anomalous" phenomenon. This requires establishing the performance envelopes of all known natural and anthropogenic phenomena. Data that falls outside these established parameters, and cannot be identified despite sufficient data quality, would constitute an anomalous dataset.

The working group must then address the difficult epistemological question of how to reason from this anomalous data. This includes considering, under a rational framework, the set of all possible hypotheses, including the challenging consideration of "technosignatures of nonhuman origin" and updating Bayesian priors based on genuinely new and extraordinary evidence.

The Ultimate Goal

The ultimate goal of this Working Group is to produce a definitive white paper on which the scientific community at large—including key players in government science like NASA and ESA—can agree. This text will form the ground rules for any future scientific determination regarding UAP.

Without such pre-established standards, the empirical and scholarly study of UAP will remain suspect in mainstream discourse. This work is both timely and essential for legitimate progress in the field.

Prospective White Paper Structure

  1. Defining Standards of Evidence in Science.
  2. What Constitutes Scientific Evidence and Data?
  3. Historical Handling of Anomalous Observations.
  4. Methodology for Designing Experiments for the Unknown.
  5. The Role of Validation and Verification in Data Analysis.
  6. How a Scientific Result is Accepted as Standard.
  7. Post-Conclusion: Communication and Societal Impact.
